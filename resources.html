Getting Support
in Singapore

Parent Truth

Knowing where to go is half the battle. The system is not always obvious.

Clinical and Assessment Services
KK Women's and Children's Hospital — Department of Child DevelopmentSG
Hospital
The primary public hospital route for developmental paediatric assessments in Singapore. Waiting times can be long but the pathway is well-established. A GP referral is usually required. Subsidised rates available for Singapore citizens and PRs.
kkh.com.sg
National University Hospital — Paediatric Psychological ServicesSG
Hospital
The western Singapore equivalent of KKH's CDU. Also requires a GP referral. Good option if you are in the west of the island or prefer NUH's network.
nuh.com.sg
Private Educational PsychologistsSG
Private
For school-age children, a private educational psychologist assessment typically costs SGD $1,500–$3,000. The advantage is shorter wait times and a more detailed written report that schools can act on. Ask your school's Learning Support Coordinator if they can recommend someone, as they will be familiar with local report formats.
There is no central registry. Ask your child's school, paediatrician, or other parents in your network for a personal recommendation.
School-Based Support
MOE Learning Support ProgrammeSG
Government
Available in all mainstream primary schools. The Learning Support Coordinator (LSC) is your first point of contact at school. They cannot diagnose, but they can observe, document, and refer. Ask your child's form teacher how to request a meeting with the LSC.
moe.gov.sg
SPED Schools in SingaporeSG
Government
For children with more significant support needs, MOE-funded SPED schools offer specialist programmes. A formal diagnosis and professional recommendation is required for referral. The full list is on the MOE website.
moe.gov.sg/special-education

Books Worth
Reading

Parent Truth

One good book at the right moment can reframe years of confusion in an afternoon.

A note on these recommendations

These are books for parents, not textbooks. They are chosen because they are readable, grounded in research, and practically useful — not because they are the most well-known titles in their category. I am not sure about all publication details so please verify before purchasing.

For Understanding Your Child's Brain
The Explosive Child — Ross W. Greene
Book
The most practically useful book on the shelf for parents whose children have frequent and intense emotional outbursts. Greene's Collaborative Problem Solving model reframes the question from "how do I get my child to comply?" to "what skills is my child missing?" A genuine shift, not a technique. Well-evidenced and humane.
Works across a wide range of profiles. You do not need a diagnosis to find it useful.
The Whole-Brain Child — Daniel J. Siegel and Tina Payne Bryson
Book
Accessible neuroscience for parents. Covers how the developing brain handles big emotions, why reasoning does not work when a child is dysregulated, and twelve strategies grounded in developmental science. One of the few popular parenting books that does not oversimplify the science.
Smart but Stuck — Thomas E. Brown
Book
Particularly useful for parents of bright children who are struggling in ways that do not make obvious sense. Brown reframes attention and executive function in a way that explains the gap between ability and performance without relying on deficit framing. Readable and non-clinical in tone.
For Building Resilience
Mindset — Carol S. Dweck
Book
The original source on growth mindset, and worth reading in full rather than via the summary you have probably already seen. Dweck is careful and specific in ways that the popularised version is not. Particularly relevant for children who have learned to avoid challenge because failure feels like identity.
The Self-Driven Child — William Stixrud and Ned Johnson
Book
Covers autonomy, stress, and academic pressure in a way that is unusually well-suited to the Singapore context. The research on parental anxiety and its transmission to children is particularly worth sitting with. Practical without being prescriptive.
One of the more useful books for parents navigating PSLE pressure alongside a child who is already struggling.

Parent Truth

Other parents who have been through this are more useful than most websites. Find your people.

Local Online Resources
Autism Resource Centre (ARC) SingaporeSG
Organisation
Provides programmes, training, and family support. Their parent workshops are regularly updated and practically oriented. Useful even if your child does not have an autism diagnosis — many of the strategies covered apply across profiles.
autism.org.sg
Unlocking ADHDSG
Community
Unlocking ADHD is an IPC-registered charity and social service agency in Singapore that started with a mission to empower ADHDers and their families. Their resources are regularly updated, and host a community support ground on Facebook and Discord.
unlockingadhd.org.sg
Online Resources Worth Bookmarking
Understood.org
Website
The most consistently reliable non-clinical resource on the internet for parents of children who learn differently. Well-researched, non-sensationalist, and updated regularly. Covers learning differences, attention, and social-emotional development. US-based but most of the content is universally applicable.
understood.org
Child Mind Institute
Website
Clinically rigorous articles on child mental health, attention, anxiety, and learning differences. Written for parents and professionals alike. More detailed than most parenting sites and less prone to oversimplification. Use the search function — the article library is extensive.
childmind.org
Ross Greene's Lives in the Balance
Website
Free resources from the Collaborative Problem Solving framework, including guides, videos, and downloadable tools. If you found The Explosive Child useful, this is where to go next.
livesinthebalance.org

Considering
an Assessment?

Parent Truth

A diagnosis does not change who your child is. It changes what help they are entitled to ask for.

What an Assessment Actually Is

A formal assessment is a structured evaluation, usually conducted by a developmental paediatrician or educational psychologist, that maps your child's cognitive, behavioural, and emotional profile. It is not a test your child passes or fails. It is a description.

The output is typically a written report that describes how your child learns, where their strengths are, where they need support, and what accommodations would help. That report is what you bring to school.

The Two Pathways in Singapore
Public route — via Polyclinic to KKH or NUHSG
Subsidised
Start with your GP and ask for a referral to the Child Development Unit at KKH or NUH. Subsidised rates apply for citizens and PRs. Waiting times are typically several months. The assessment itself is thorough and the reports are well-recognised by MOE and mainstream schools.
Bring specific observations to the GP — what you see at home, how often, in what contexts. Vague descriptions produce longer referral delays.
Private route — Educational PsychologistSG
Private, SGD $1,500–$3,000
Faster, more detailed, and more expensive. The report format is usually more school-actionable. Ask your child's school Learning Support Coordinator for a recommendation — they know which reports are taken seriously and which are not. Ask the psychologist directly how many pages their report is and what it covers before booking.
Questions Worth Asking Before You Start
Does my child need a diagnosis to get school support?
Question
In Singapore mainstream schools, no. A Learning Support Coordinator can put informal support in place based on teacher observation. A diagnosis strengthens the case and unlocks more formal accommodations, including exam access arrangements, but it is not required to start the conversation.
What do I do with the report once I have it?
Question
Request a meeting with your child's form teacher and LSC. Bring the report. Ask specifically what accommodations the school can put in place and what the timeline is. Schools vary significantly in how proactively they act on reports. You may need to follow up more than once.
Should I tell my child about the assessment?
Question
Generally, yes — and in age-appropriate terms. Children who understand that the assessment is about getting them better support, not labelling what is wrong with them, tend to engage more honestly with the process. The Diagnosis Conversation card in the Moment Cards section has specific language for this.
The First Step

If you are seriously considering an assessment, write down three specific things you have observed in the last month — not general impressions, but specific moments. That list is what you bring to the GP or psychologist. It will save you time and produce a better outcome.
