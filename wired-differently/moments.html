About these cards

These cards are for the moment after something hard happens. Not for when everything is calm and you have time to reflect. For right now, when you are standing in the kitchen and nothing is working and you need to know what to say.

Each one names a hard moment, explains what is actually happening neurologically, and gives you three ways to respond. There is no perfect script. There is just a direction.

One card at a time is enough.

These cards are educational and do not replace clinical assessment, therapy, or medical advice. If you have concerns about your child's development or wellbeing, please speak to a qualified professional.

01
Moment Card One

Homework
Shutdown

Parent Truth

If homework time has become the part of the day you dread most, you are not the only parent living like that.

The Moment

Your child sits down to do homework. And just stops. Won't open the book. Won't pick up the pen. Sometimes cries. Sometimes gets aggressive. Sometimes just stares. You have asked three times. Nothing.

What's Happening

The brain has hit a wall called task initiation. This is the neurological ability to begin something that feels effortful, uncertain, or unstimulating, and for many neurodiverse children it is a genuine gap in executive function, not a choice. Add a full school day of holding it together and by 4pm there is often nothing left. The meltdown is not about the homework. It is the bill coming due for a day of enormous invisible effort.

First Things First

Take one breath. What looks like defiance is usually depletion.

What Not To Say

"You've been sitting there for twenty minutes doing nothing. It's just homework."

Accurate. Completely useless to a brain that cannot locate a starting point, and likely to escalate what is already a fragile moment.

What To Say
Calm

"You don't have to do all of it. Just find the page. That's the only job right now."

Warm

"I can see this feels really hard right now. Let's just sit together for a bit. You don't have to start yet."

Firm

"I know your brain is tired. We're not skipping it, but we're going to make it smaller. What's the tiniest piece we can start with?"

One Small Next Step

Tomorrow, delay homework for twenty minutes after school. Then make the first task absurdly small: open the book, find the page, or write the name.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Neurodiverse children often expend significantly more cognitive and emotional energy than their peers just getting through a standard school day. Research on ADHD and autism consistently describes elevated mental effort required to manage attention, behaviour, and social demands in mainstream classroom settings. The shutdown you see at homework time is frequently the delayed crash. Not defiance, not laziness, and not a reflection of how they feel about you.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

02
Moment Card Two

The Hyperactive
Child

Parent Truth

You can understand why your child moves constantly and still feel completely exhausted by it.

The Moment

Your child cannot sit still. Touching everything, bouncing off furniture, talking over people, interrupting constantly. School is sending notes home. You are exhausted. And underneath the exhaustion is something harder: you know they are not doing it on purpose, but you cannot always hold onto that in the middle of it.

What's Happening

Hyperactivity in neurodiverse children is not a behaviour choice. It is the nervous system regulating itself the only way it knows how. Movement increases dopamine and norepinephrine, the brain chemicals responsible for attention and impulse control. When those chemicals are chronically low, the brain recruits the body to help. The fidgeting, the pacing, the constant motion: that is the brain self-medicating, not misbehaving.

First Things First

Ask yourself: is this moment actually a problem, or does it just look like one?

What Not To Say

"Why can't you just sit still like everyone else?"

That comparison does not motivate. It teaches your child that their brain is the wrong kind.

What To Say
Calm

"Your body needs to move. Let's do two minutes of jumping before we sit down. Then we'll try again."

Warm

"I know sitting still feels almost impossible sometimes. That's your brain, not you being naughty. Let's figure out what helps."

Firm

"I need you to bring your body to calm. You can sit on the floor, stand at the table, or use your fidget. You choose. But we need to start now."

One Small Next Step

Before asking for focus, give the body movement on purpose: jumping, pushing against a wall, carrying something heavy, or animal walks. Two minutes counts.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Movement is not the enemy of focus for many neurodiverse children. It is the entry point. A child who is allowed to move often focuses better than one who is forced to be still. If your child's school is penalising movement as a default response, that is worth a conversation with the teacher.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

03
Moment Card Three

Inattentive
in Class

Parent Truth

Many parents spend years wondering whether their child is struggling or simply not trying hard enough. That uncertainty is painful.

The Moment

The teacher says your child is daydreaming, missing instructions, losing things. They are not disruptive. Just absent. The phrase you keep hearing is "not reaching their potential." You have started to wonder whether your child is simply not trying, or whether something else is going on.

What's Happening

Inattentive presentation is one of the most frequently missed profiles in Singapore classrooms because it causes no disruption. These children are not ignoring instructions. Their brain's attention regulation system is genuinely inconsistent, drifting not out of boredom but because the neural circuits that anchor attention require more stimulation or novelty to stay engaged. The child who cannot pay attention is not the same as the child who will not.

First Things First

Resist the urge to lead with the teacher's feedback. Your child has probably already received it and is carrying it quietly.

What Not To Say

"The teacher says you're not paying attention. You need to try harder."

A child who is neurologically struggling to regulate attention cannot try their way out of it. This sentence adds shame to an already depleting experience.

What To Say
Calm

"Your teacher mentioned you've been finding it hard to concentrate. That doesn't mean anything is wrong with you. Can you tell me what it feels like when your brain drifts off?"

Warm

"I know school can feel really long sometimes. I'm not cross. I just want to understand what's hard so we can figure it out together."

Firm

"I hear that it's hard to focus. And I also need you to try something new with me this week. Just one thing. Are you up for it?"

What To Say To School
For the teacher conversation

Teachers often receive this kind of conversation defensively. Lead with curiosity, not complaint.

"We've noticed [child] finds it hard to sustain focus, especially on tasks that feel repetitive. We'd love to understand what you're seeing and explore whether any small adjustments to their environment or instructions might help. We're not asking for less rigour. We're trying to figure out the right conditions."

One Small Next Step

Pick one external attention support this week: a visual checklist, timer, or fixed homework spot. Do not change five things at once.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Children with inattentive profiles are often quietly exhausted by the effort of trying to pay attention in environments not designed for their brain. What looks like switched-off is frequently working very hard and losing the battle.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

04
Moment Card Four

Transitions
and Change

Parent Truth

It is hard to stay calm when a tiny change becomes a family-wide crisis for the third time this week.

The Moment

A plan changed. The substitute teacher showed up. You said five more minutes and then it was time to leave. And your child fell apart completely. The reaction seems wildly out of proportion to what happened. Other people are staring. You do not know whether to comfort or correct.

What's Happening

For many neurodiverse children, transitions are genuine neurological events. The brain relies on predictability to manage anxiety and regulate the nervous system. When the expected sequence changes, the amygdala, the brain's threat detection system, can respond as though something dangerous has happened. This is not drama or manipulation. Cognitive flexibility, the ability to shift mental gears smoothly, is a known difficulty in both ADHD and autism, and one of the last executive functions to fully mature.

First Things First

Do not try to reason with a dysregulated nervous system. Get alongside it first.

What Not To Say

"It's not a big deal. Why do you always do this?"

The size of the reaction is not a choice. Calling it disproportionate in the moment escalates it.

What To Say
Calm

"I know that wasn't what you expected. That's really hard when your brain likes to know what's coming. Let's take a minute."

Warm

"The change surprised your brain, didn't it. I've got you. We can be upset about it for a bit, and then we'll figure out the next step together."

Firm

"I know this isn't what we planned. We still need to move. I'm going to count to five slowly, and then we go. I'll hold your hand."

What To Say To School
For the teacher conversation

Teachers often receive this kind of conversation defensively. Lead with curiosity, not complaint.

"We've found that [child] handles changes to routine significantly better when they have some advance warning. Even a two-minute heads-up before transitions makes a real difference. Would it be possible to flag any changes at the start of the day? We're not asking for a different curriculum, just a bit of predictability around the schedule."

One Small Next Step

Start using countdown warnings before transitions: ten minutes, five minutes, two minutes. Predictability reduces panic.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Predictability is not a preference for many neurodiverse children. It is a neurological need. Visual schedules, advance warning, and explicitly narrating upcoming changes are not spoiling. They are accommodations that reduce the number of times the alarm system gets triggered unnecessarily.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

05
Moment Card Five

The Sensory
Flashpoint

Parent Truth

Trying to explain sensory overwhelm to people who cannot see it can become exhausting and lonely for parents too.

The Moment

The school uniform collar is wrong. The food at the canteen smells unbearable. The shopping centre is too loud. The tag in the shirt has been a problem since 7am. To everyone around you, this looks like a tantrum. You know it is something else, but you cannot always explain it, even to yourself.

What's Happening

Sensory processing differences are common in neurodiverse children and significantly underrecognised. The brain's sensory filtering system works differently in many children with ADHD and autism. Stimuli that register as background noise to most people, a seam, a smell, a frequency of light, can register as genuinely overwhelming or even painful. Research estimates suggest the majority of children with autism experience clinically significant sensory differences, with some studies citing figures as high as 90%. Sensory differences are also frequently reported in children with ADHD, though prevalence figures vary across studies and diagnostic criteria. The shirt tag is not trivial to this child's brain.

First Things First

Remove the trigger if you can. The conversation comes after, not during.

What Not To Say

"You're being ridiculous. Nobody else is complaining about the food."

Other children's sensory thresholds have no bearing on your child's nervous system. This sentence is factually irrelevant and emotionally isolating.

What To Say
Calm

"That's really bothering your body, isn't it. Let's fix the thing first. We can talk about the rest in a minute."

Warm

"I believe you that it hurts. Some brains feel things more strongly than others. Yours is one of them. That's not wrong, it's just how you're made."

Firm

"I can see your body is really uncomfortable. We need to get through the next ten minutes. What would help most right now: quiet, space, or fixing the thing that's bothering you?"

One Small Next Step

Notice patterns for one week. What triggers your child most often: noise, clothing, smells, crowds, hunger, or tiredness? Patterns matter more than isolated incidents.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Sensory sensitivities tend to worsen when the child is already tired, hungry, or stressed. A child who tolerates the uniform fine on a good day may genuinely not be able to on a hard one. The variation is not inconsistency. It is the cumulative load showing.

A note: If sensory difficulties are significantly affecting your child's daily life at school or at home, an occupational therapist with experience in sensory processing is the right next step. This is not an overreaction. It is the appropriate tool for this level of need.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

06
Moment Card Six

Social Cues
and Friendships

Parent Truth

Watching your child want connection and repeatedly miss it can be heartbreaking in a very quiet way.

The Moment

Your child talks over people, misses the joke everyone else got, doesn't notice when someone wants the conversation to end. They come home saying nobody wants to play with them. You are watching them try hard and land wrong, repeatedly, and it is breaking your heart a little.

What's Happening

Reading social cues requires a rapid, largely unconscious process of interpreting facial expressions, body language, tone, and timing simultaneously. For many neurodiverse children, this process is not automatic. It requires conscious effort, which is slow and prone to error in fast-moving group situations. Research on autism and empathy suggests that many children with autism experience strong emotional empathy and genuine desire for connection. What is more consistently different is the automatic, unconscious reading of unspoken social rules that neurotypical children absorb almost effortlessly. The motivation is there. The neurological toolkit for decoding the invisible rulebook often is not.

First Things First

Don't problem-solve immediately. They need to feel heard before they can hear advice.

What Not To Say

"Just be yourself and the right friends will come."

Genuinely well-meaning. Often untrue for neurodiverse children who are trying to be themselves and finding that it keeps not working.

What To Say
Calm

"That sounds really lonely. Can you tell me what happened from the beginning? I want to understand the whole thing."

Warm

"I'm really glad you told me. Making friends can be genuinely hard, and it's even harder when your brain works differently. That doesn't mean there's no one out there who gets you. It means we need to find your people."

Firm

"I hear you. Can we think together about one moment that felt off, and what you might try differently next time? Not because it was your fault, but because there are things we can practise."

One Small Next Step

After a social moment goes badly, resist immediate advice. Start with: do you want comfort, help figuring it out, or both?

Why this happens the science, briefly

Social skills can be explicitly taught. What neurotypical children absorb passively often needs to be made conscious and practised deliberately for neurodiverse children. This is not remediation. It is giving your child a map to territory everyone else seems to navigate by instinct.

A note: If your child's social difficulties are causing significant distress or isolation, a psychologist or social skills group designed for neurodiverse children can make a meaningful difference. Home practice is a strong start. It is not always enough on its own.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

07
moment Card Seven

"I'm Stupid" /
Self-Worth Collapse

Parent Truth

Many parents panic in this moment because they suddenly realise how much pain their child has been carrying silently.

The Moment

After a bad test result, a social rejection, a hard afternoon. Your child says it. "I'm stupid." "I can't do anything right." "I wish I wasn't like this." The words land like a punch and you freeze, not sure whether to contradict, comfort, investigate, or panic.

What's Happening

Negative self-talk in neurodiverse children is often the accumulated residue of repeated experiences of trying hard and still falling short. By the time a child says "I'm stupid," they have usually gathered significant evidence, in their own mind, that supports that conclusion. Clinical guidance on neurodiversity disclosure notes that children without accurate language for their differences will generate their own explanations, and those explanations are almost always harsher than the truth.

First Things First

Don't immediately contradict. "No you're not, you're so smart" closes the conversation before it opens.

What Not To Say

"Don't say that, you're brilliant."

Loving, instinctive, and often counterproductive. A child in self-worth collapse is not asking for reassurance. They are asking to be understood.

What To Say
Calm

"That sounds like a really hard thought to be carrying. When did you start feeling like that? I want to understand it, not just argue with it."

Warm

"I hear you. And I don't want to just tell you you're wrong, because I know it doesn't feel wrong to you right now. Can you tell me what happened today that made it feel so true?"

Firm

"That's a big thing to say about yourself. I don't agree with it. But I also don't want to just say 'no you're not' and move on. Let's talk about what's actually hard and what we're going to do about it."

One Small Next Step

Tonight, name one specific thing your child handled well today that had nothing to do with grades or achievement.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Neurodiverse children are at significantly higher risk of anxiety and low self-esteem, not because of their neurotype, but because of the repeated experience of a world not built for their brain. Moments like this one are worth taking seriously. If they become frequent, speaking to a professional is not an overreaction. It is the right call.

A note: If your child is expressing hopelessness, persistent sadness, or statements about not wanting to exist, please speak to your paediatrician or a child psychologist. This goes beyond what a parent tool can or should address alone.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

08
Moment Card Eight

Singled Out
in Class

Parent Truth

It is possible to feel angry with the school, protective of your child, and unsure what really happened all at the same time.

The Moment

Your child is being called out publicly. Name on the board. Moved to a separate seat. Asked to leave the room. They come home either shut down or furious. You are trying to stay neutral but underneath you are angry, and also not entirely sure whose side to be on, which makes you feel guilty.

What's Happening

Public correction activates the shame response in all children. In neurodiverse children, particularly those with rejection sensitive dysphoria, a trait strongly associated with ADHD, the experience of being singled out can be genuinely traumatic rather than merely embarrassing. Dr William Dodson, an ADHD specialist, describes rejection sensitive dysphoria as one of the most impairing yet least recognised aspects of ADHD. A child who overreacts to being called out may not be being dramatic. Their nervous system is experiencing something several times more intense than a neurotypical child would.

First Things First

Your child needs to know you are on their side before they can hear any nuance about the teacher's perspective.

What Not To Say

"The teacher's just doing their job. You need to behave better."

Even if partially true, said first it communicates that you are not on their side. That is the thing they will remember.

What To Say
Calm

"That sounds really humiliating. I'm not going to tell you how you should feel about it. Can you tell me exactly what happened?"

Warm

"Being called out in front of everyone is awful. I would have hated that too. I'm glad you told me. You're not in trouble with me."

Firm

"I hear you and I believe it felt really bad. I'm going to speak to your teacher. And I also need to ask: is there anything that happened before that moment that I should know about? Not to blame you. Just so I understand the full picture."

What To Say To School
For the teacher conversation

Teachers often receive this kind of conversation defensively. Lead with curiosity, not complaint.

"We've noticed that being corrected publicly has a significant impact on [child]. They find it genuinely difficult to recover from, and it sometimes affects the rest of their day. We're not trying to remove accountability, we just wonder if there might be a way to address things more quietly when possible. We'd love to understand your perspective on what's been happening."

One Small Next Step

Before speaking to the school, write down what your child felt, what the teacher likely saw, and what outcome you actually want.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Children with rejection sensitive dysphoria often describe the experience of being criticised as physically painful. Research on social pain suggests that emotional rejection and physical pain share overlapping neural pathways, which may partly explain why the distress feels bodily rather than merely emotional. Taking your child's distress seriously is not indulging them. It is accurate.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

09
Moment Card Nine

The
Sibling Gap

Parent Truth

Almost every parent compares their children sometimes. The important thing is noticing it before your child starts believing the comparison too.

The Moment

One child seems to sail through school. The other struggles visibly. Your neurodiverse child has started to notice. They make comments. Compare results. Ask why school is easy for their sibling and not for them. And sometimes, on your hardest days, you catch yourself making the comparison too, even briefly, and then you feel terrible about it.

What's Happening

Sibling comparison is painful for any child. For a neurodiverse child already working significantly harder than their peers just to keep up, it carries an additional weight. Research on sibling dynamics in families with a neurodiverse child notes that resentment can build in both directions when the family does not explicitly address the difference. The neurodiverse child can feel like a burden. The neurotypical sibling can feel overlooked. The family conversation about different kinds of brains is one of the most protective things you can have.

First Things First

Do not rush to reassure. Do not compare out loud, in either direction. Do not pretend the difference is not there.

What Not To Say

"Your sister manages fine. Why can't you just try harder?"

Even once is enough for that sentence to live in a child's memory for years.

What To Say
Calm

"You've noticed that some things feel harder for you than they do for your brother or sister. You're right. They are harder. And I want to explain why, because it's actually really interesting."

Warm

"Different brains are good at different things. Your sister's brain finds sitting still and remembering instructions easy. Your brain finds other things easy that are hard for her. Neither of you got the better deal. You just got different ones."

Firm

"I know it feels unfair sometimes. It is unfair in some ways. And the thing I want you to know is that hard does not mean impossible. Your brain is going to need different tools. We're going to find them."

One Small Next Step

This week, spend ten minutes alone with each child separately. No correcting. No teaching. Just attention without comparison.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Effort is invisible. A neurodiverse child who produces average results may have worked three times as hard as the sibling who produced excellent ones. Recognising effort explicitly, and separately from outcome, is one of the most powerful things a parent can do for a child who keeps trying and keeps finding it hard.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

Moment Card Ten

Rough Hands
at School

Parent Truth

It is possible to feel protective of your child and horrified by what happened at the same time.

The Moment

The school called. Your child hit someone, pushed someone, hurt someone. You are mortified. You are also, underneath the mortification, aware that this does not fit the child you know at home. Or maybe it does, and that is its own kind of frightening. Either way, you have to respond to the school, respond to your child, and figure out what actually happened, all at the same time.

What's Happening

Physical aggression in neurodiverse children is almost always a communication and dysregulation issue, not a character one. When the brain's threat detection system is chronically over-sensitive, ordinary frustrations can cross the threshold into fight response faster and more unpredictably. The hit or push is the nervous system's emergency exit when words have failed or are not yet available. Research on aggression in children with ADHD consistently shows the behaviour is impulsive rather than premeditated. The child who hits is often the child who felt cornered, humiliated, or overwhelmed first.

First Things First

To yourself: this is a skill problem, not a character verdict. To your child: not yet. Get the facts first.

What Not To Say

"You've embarrassed this whole family."

Shame does not build impulse control. It adds another overwhelming feeling to a child who is already overwhelmed, and closes down the conversation you actually need to have.

What To Say
Calm

"I've heard what happened. I'm not going to shout. I need you to tell me what was going on for you before it happened. Start from the beginning."

Warm

"I know you're probably feeling a lot of things right now. I'm not here to pile on. I just need to understand what happened so we can figure out how to do it differently next time."

Firm

"What happened today is not okay and we're going to talk about it seriously. And I also need to understand what was happening in your body before you hit. Because that's where we need to start if we're going to fix this."

What To Say To School
For the teacher conversation

Teachers often receive this kind of conversation defensively. Lead with accountability first, then context.

"We take what happened seriously and we're working on it at home. We're also trying to understand what was happening for [child] before it escalated, because if we can identify the trigger earlier, we might be able to prevent it. Is there anything in the lead-up that you noticed? We want to work on this together."

One Small Next Step

Later, in a calm moment, ask: what does your body feel like right before you explode? Start helping your child notice the warning signs earlier.

Why this happens the science, briefly

Many neurodiverse children who display aggression are also experiencing significant anxiety underneath it. The aggression is the visible surface of a much larger internal experience. If this is a recurring pattern, seeking support from a professional who understands both the neurological and emotional dimensions is not a failure. It is a child whose needs have outgrown what any parent can address alone.

A note: If physical aggression is happening regularly, a psychologist with experience in behaviour and neurodiversity is the appropriate next step. Home strategies matter and are not sufficient on their own when the pattern is established.

Regulated parents are not parents who never lose patience. They are parents who repair after hard moments.

11
Moment Card Eleven

The Diagnosis
Conversation

Parent Truth

Many parents grieve quietly after a diagnosis, even when the diagnosis also brings relief and clarity.

The Moment

You have a report. Or you are about to. There is now a word, or several words, for what you have both been living with. And now you have to decide how to tell them. When. How much. Whether they are ready. Whether you are ready. You have been carrying this for days and it is heavy.

What's Happening

Every instinct to protect your child from this information is understandable and, according to clinical guidance, counterproductive. Children who are told about their diagnosis in age-appropriate language consistently do better than those who are not. They develop self-compassion faster, stop blaming themselves, and gain language for an experience they have already been living without words for. The child already knows something is different. They have known for a long time. What they do not yet have is an explanation that removes the blame from themselves.

First Things First

Find a moment that is calm, private, and unhurried. Not after a hard day. Not before school. Choose a time when your child feels safe and you feel steady.

What Not To Say

"Please don't tell anyone. It's private."

Said with the best intentions, this teaches a child that their diagnosis is something shameful. Instead: "This is your information and you get to decide who you tell."

What To Say
Calm

"I want to tell you something that I think is going to help you understand yourself a bit better. You know how some things feel harder for you than they seem to for other kids? There's actually a reason for that. Your brain is wired a bit differently. And that has a name."

Warm

"You know how we've talked about how your brain sometimes makes things harder? I've been finding out more about why that is, and I want to share it with you because I think it's actually good news. It means it was never your fault. It was never because you weren't trying hard enough. Your brain just works in a way that needs different things."

One Small Next Step

You do not need one perfect conversation. Start smaller than you think. One calm, honest explanation is enough for the first time.

Why this matters the science, briefly

This is not a single conversation. It is the first one. Clinical guidance is consistent: disclosure is an ongoing dialogue that evolves as your child grows, asks new questions, and encounters new situations where their diagnosis becomes relevant. You are not delivering a verdict. You are opening a door. Children who have language for their differences are significantly more likely to develop self-advocacy, seek support when they need it, and maintain positive self-esteem through adolescence.

A note: Before or after the diagnosis conversation, speaking with the psychologist or paediatrician who conducted the assessment is strongly recommended. They can advise on how to tailor the conversation for your specific child's age, profile, and readiness. You do not have to do this alone.
