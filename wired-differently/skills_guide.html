About this guide

Some children run fast and hot. Others drift, or find noise overwhelming, or want friends badly and keep landing wrong. These children are not broken versions of easier children. They are children whose nervous systems need different conditions to work well.

Each skill here is designed to be practised in small, repeated doses during ordinary moments — not deployed for the first time during the worst five minutes of the day. That's how the brain actually builds durable habits.

One try is a start. Twenty tries is a skill.

This guide is a companion to the Flashpoint Cards. It is educational and does not replace assessment, therapy, medical care, or individualised school support.

01
Skill One

Task
Initiation

Parent Truth

It is exhausting to stay calm when the worksheet is right there and somehow still nothing is happening.

What this helps with

Task initiation is the ability to begin something that feels effortful, uncertain, repetitive, or emotionally loaded. Many neurodiverse children do not refuse homework because they lack intelligence or because they enjoy making the evening worse for everyone in the kitchen. They may be stuck at the starting point because planning, sequencing, emotional regulation, and working memory are all being asked to come online at once.

The aim is to make the first action so clear and so small that the child's brain can begin before shame, panic, or argument takes over.

Try this tonight

Do not start with "Finish your homework." Sit beside your child, lower the demand, and make the first step visible. You might say, "We are not doing the whole thing yet. We are only opening the worksheet and finding question one." Once that first action is done, pause briefly and name it: "You started. That was the hard part." Then offer the next tiny step.

How to do it
  1. Choose one task only. Put the rest of the homework pile aside so the child is not looking at the entire mountain.
  2. Make the first step physical and visible — opening the book, pointing to question one, reading one sentence, or writing the date.
  3. Stay nearby for the first three minutes without correcting handwriting, posture, speed, or attitude unless safety is involved.
  4. After the first step, reflect success calmly: "Your brain found the starting point."
  5. Offer the next step in the same small format. Continue until the child has enough momentum to work more independently.
What to say
Calm

"We are only finding the first step. Nothing else yet."

Warm

"I can see your brain is stuck. I will sit with you while it gets going."

Firm

"We are not skipping it, but we are making it smaller."

Why this works the science, briefly

Breaking a task into concrete first actions reduces working memory demand and lowers emotional threat. Co-regulation also matters because children often organise themselves more effectively when a calm adult is physically nearby. These strategies are consistent with behavioural parent training, executive function scaffolding, and evidence-informed approaches used for ADHD and related developmental differences.

02
Skill Two

Movement &
Regulation

Parent Truth

You can understand why your child needs movement and still feel completely finished by the tenth bounce off the sofa.

What this helps with

Movement is not always the opposite of attention. For many children with ADHD, sensory processing differences, or high arousal levels, movement helps the nervous system organise itself. The child who is pacing, tapping, chewing, or wobbling may not be ignoring you. They may be trying to stay alert enough to listen.

The skill is not to allow chaos. The skill is to separate helpful movement from unsafe movement, and to give the body appropriate ways to regulate before the situation becomes a full family circus.

Try this tonight

Before asking for homework, dinner-table sitting, or a difficult conversation, give the body movement on purpose. Say, "Your body needs a reset before we start. Choose wall pushes, twenty jumps, or carrying the laundry basket." Keep it short, concrete, and predictable. Movement works best when it is offered before the child is already over the edge.

How to do it
  1. Pick two or three safe movement options your child can use at home — wall pushes, jumping, animal walks, stretching, or carrying something heavy.
  2. Use the same phrase each time: "Movement first, then we try the task." Predictability reduces negotiation.
  3. Set a clear limit — two minutes or twenty jumps — so the movement has a beginning and end.
  4. After movement, move straight into the next tiny task before the child drifts into something else.
  5. If the movement becomes unsafe, name the boundary without shaming the need: "Your body can move. It cannot crash into people."
What to say
Calm

"Your body needs movement before your brain can focus."

Warm

"This is not you being naughty. Your body is trying to organise itself."

Firm

"Movement is allowed. Hurting people or breaking things is not."

Why this works the science, briefly

Evidence from ADHD and occupational therapy-informed practice supports the use of movement, proprioceptive input, and environmental adaptations to improve regulation and task engagement for some children. This does not replace individual occupational therapy where sensory issues are significant, but it gives parents a safe and practical starting point.

03
Skill Three

Attention
Anchoring

Parent Truth

It is hard not to take it personally when you have said the same instruction five times and your child looks genuinely surprised every time.

What this helps with

Attention anchoring helps children whose focus drifts, instructions disappear, or belongings seem to enter a small private black hole somewhere between the classroom and the school gate. The point is not to tell the child to "pay attention" more often. The point is to make attention easier to hold by using external cues, shorter instructions, predictable routines, and connection before correction.

Children with attention differences often face this paradox both ways. Their focus drifts during a maths explanation — but that same brain can lock onto a game for two solid hours with no awareness that anyone has called their name four times from the kitchen. Both are regulation difficulties pulling in opposite directions.

Try this tonight

Choose one repeating routine, such as getting ready for school, starting homework, or packing the bag. Write three steps only on a card or whiteboard. Use the same words every time, such as "Shoes, bottle, bag." Stand near your child, point to the list, and ask them to check off each step rather than relying on repeated verbal reminders.

When your child is locked in

Don't call from across the room. Walk into the space, come down to their level, and make gentle physical contact before you speak. If you can, give one warning before the hyperfocus starts — "twenty minutes, then we stop" — not only when time is already up. When it is time to finish, offer a clear next step immediately: "After you save, we go straight to snack." The transition is easier when there is somewhere to land.

How to do it
  1. Pick one routine that causes daily friction. Do not try to fix every routine in the house at once.
  2. Reduce it to three to five visible steps and write them in the order they need to happen.
  3. Place the list where the task happens, not somewhere decorative and useless.
  4. Use fewer words when prompting. Point to the list and say, "Check the next step."
  5. Review what worked after the routine, not during the meltdown. Adjust the list if one step is too vague.
  6. If your child is locked in, enter the physical space before you speak. A voice from another room will not reach a brain that is fully absorbed. One tap, one calm instruction, one clear next step.
What to say
Calm

"Let's let the list hold the steps so your brain does not have to."

Warm

"Forgetting does not mean you do not care. It means we need a better system."

Note

When locked in, the firm approach does not apply. Enter the space, stay calm, one sentence.

Why this works the science, briefly

Visual cues, routines, checklists, and reduced verbal load are widely used executive function supports. They reduce demands on working memory and help children shift from adult-dependent prompting toward environmental cues they can eventually use more independently.

04
Skill Four

Transitions &
Structure

Parent Truth

It is hard to stay patient when a tiny change has somehow become the main event of the evening.

What this helps with

Transitions are not simply a matter of cooperation. They require the brain to stop one state, accept a new expectation, regulate disappointment, shift attention, and begin the next action. For children with anxiety, sensory vulnerabilities, or nervous systems that need more time to shift gears, that is a lot to ask in one sudden sentence shouted from the doorway.

Building structure and predictability into transitions gives the child more warning, more clarity, and fewer unnecessary surprises, so change becomes more manageable rather than repeatedly feeling like a threat.

Try this tonight

Choose one difficult transition and use a three-step warning system. For example, before leaving the playground, say, "Ten minutes left," then "Five minutes left," then "Two more turns and we go." Use the same sequence each time. When it is time to leave, avoid explaining endlessly. Repeat the final boundary calmly and move into action.

How to do it
  1. Identify one transition that regularly causes distress — leaving the house, ending screen time, bedtime, or school pickup.
  2. Create a predictable warning sequence using time, turns, or tasks. Choose the version your child understands best.
  3. Use a visual timer if time is too abstract. Name what happens when it ends before the activity starts — not after. "When the timer runs out, shoes go on."
  4. At the final warning, offer a simple next action: "Shoes on now" or "Walk to the gate now."
  5. After the transition, briefly acknowledge effort: "That change was hard, and you moved through it."
What to say
Calm

"Your brain works better when it knows what is coming. Let's decide what will happen next."

Warm

"I know leaving is hard when you were not ready. I will help you through the change."

Firm

"The timer has ended. We are going now, and I will stay with you."

Why this works the science, briefly

Visual schedules, countdowns, first-then language, and predictable routines are well-established supports for children who struggle with cognitive flexibility, anxiety, and transitions. These strategies reduce uncertainty and support regulation before behaviour escalates.

05
Skill Five

Social Scripts &
Perspective Taking

Parent Truth

Watching your child want connection and repeatedly miss it can hurt in a very quiet, very parental way.

What this helps with

Some children want friendship very badly and still keep landing awkwardly. They talk over others, miss subtle cues, do not know how to join a game, or cannot tell when a conversation has already ended for everyone else involved. The pain is often not that they do not care. It is that the social world is full of invisible rules other children seem to learn without being directly taught.

Some children miss the cues entirely. Others read the room and still can't slow down enough to act on what they notice. Both land awkwardly. Both feel the gap. The strategies here work for either, because the goal is the same: making the invisible rules more visible.

Try this tonight

Use one recent social moment, but do not start with correction. Begin with, "Do you want comfort, help figuring it out, or both?" If your child is open to help, replay only one small part of the interaction. Ask what they noticed, what the other child might have felt, and what sentence they could try next time. Practise one starter together: "Can I join in?" / "What are you playing?" / "I have an idea — want to hear it?"

How to do it
  1. Wait until your child is calm. Social learning rarely works while they are embarrassed or flooded.
  2. Choose one moment only. Do not analyse the whole friendship history like a courtroom inquiry.
  3. Ask what happened from your child's point of view before offering your interpretation.
  4. Gently add perspective: "I wonder if Sam thought you were not interested when you walked away."
  5. Practise one specific sentence for next time, such as "Can I join?" or "Do you want to keep playing or do something else?"
  6. If your child tends to interrupt, focus on one repair sentence: "I think I cut you off — what were you saying?" Practise it at home until it comes easily.
What to say
Calm

"Let's replay one part, not to blame you, but to understand it better."

Warm

"Friendship has hidden rules. We can practise them together."

Firm

"Your feelings matter, and other people's feelings matter too."

Why this works the science, briefly

Social communication support works best when it is explicit, low-pressure, and rehearsed during calm rather than reviewed in shame after a social failure. If your child's social difficulties are significant or causing real distress, ask your school counsellor or a developmental psychologist whether a structured social skills programme might help.

Skill Six

Reframing
Self-Talk

Parent Truth

It is frightening to hear your child talk about themselves in a way no parent would ever choose for them.

What this helps with

When a child says "I'm stupid" or "I ruin everything," the sentence can land like a punch. Most parents instinctively rush in with reassurance, but children in shame often cannot absorb broad praise that contradicts how true the thought feels in that moment. Reframing self-talk is not about forcing positivity. It is about helping the child build a more accurate story.

The goal is to hold two truths together: some things are genuinely hard, and hard does not mean hopeless.

It is also worth knowing that children build their internal voice partly from what they hear most often. A child who is corrected far more than they are noticed — at home, at school, across the whole day — will start to narrate themselves in the language of their mistakes.

Try this tonight

When your child says something harsh about themselves, do not immediately argue. First acknowledge the feeling, then separate the struggle from identity. You might say, "That sounds like a really painful thought. I can see maths felt awful today. But finding maths hard is not the same as being stupid."

How to do it
Not this

Rushing in with "No you're not, you're so smart!" The child already knows how the thought feels in that moment. Arguing with it before acknowledging it just means they stop listening — and possibly start hiding the thought from you next time.

  1. Listen for global labels such as "stupid," "lazy," "bad," "annoying," or "useless."
  2. Validate the feeling without agreeing with the label: "It makes sense that you feel awful after that."
  3. Name the specific difficulty: "The hard part was starting the writing task."
  4. Add one piece of evidence that widens the picture: "You kept trying even after you got upset."
  5. Repeat this pattern over time. Identity changes through many accurate repetitions, not one perfect speech.
  6. Pay attention to the balance across a full day. For every two corrections, look for one specific genuine positive — not "good job," something real.
What to say
Calm

"That is a painful thought. I want to understand it before we challenge it."

Warm

"Your struggles are real. Your strengths are real too."

Firm

"I will not let one hard moment become your whole story."

Why this works the science, briefly

This approach draws on cognitive behavioural principles, which help children identify unhelpful thoughts, examine evidence, and develop more balanced interpretations. For neurodiverse children, reframing must remain honest. False praise is usually less effective than specific, believable evidence of effort, skill, kindness, or growth.

07
Skill Seven

Repair &
Recovery

Parent Truth

It is possible to want your child to take responsibility and also hate seeing them crushed by shame.

What this helps with

Children who are corrected frequently can begin to experience all feedback as evidence of a fundamental problem with who they are, not just what they did. Repair and recovery skills help them separate the two: what happened is not the same as who I am. Self-advocacy gives them language to explain what helps, rather than relying forever on adults to translate their needs.

This skill matters because children who understand their needs are generally better able to seek support, repair mistakes, and stay connected to their own worth.

Try this tonight

After a difficult moment, separate the behaviour from the child. Say, "What happened was not okay, and you are not a bad kid. We are going to repair this and figure out what support you needed earlier." This keeps accountability intact without turning the whole conversation into a character verdict.

How to do it
Not this

"You always do this" or "Why can't you just control yourself?" These turn the repair conversation into a character verdict — and don't leave your child anywhere useful to go. Name the behaviour. Not the child.

  1. Name the behaviour clearly and calmly: "You shouted at your sister." Avoid global labels such as rude, bad, or impossible.
  2. Name belonging just as clearly: "You are still loved and still part of this family."
  3. Guide repair: "What can we do to make this right?" Offer choices if your child is stuck.
  4. Identify the unmet need or trigger: "Were you overloaded, embarrassed, rushed, or needing help?"
  5. Practise one self-advocacy sentence for next time: "I need a break," "Please say it one step at a time," or "The noise is too much."
What to say
Calm

"This is a behaviour we need to fix, not proof that you are bad."

Warm

"You can make a mistake and still belong here."

Firm

"Support is allowed. Avoiding repair is not."

Why this works the science, briefly

Behavioural and social-emotional interventions commonly emphasise clear expectations, repair, emotional literacy, and skill replacement rather than shame. Self-advocacy is also a recognised protective skill for neurodiverse children because it supports help-seeking, autonomy, and appropriate accommodation use over time.

Skill Eight

Family Strength
Narrative

Parent Truth

Almost every parent compares their children sometimes. The important thing is not letting the comparison become the family language.

What this helps with

Every family has an unspoken story about what counts as success, intelligence, effort, and being "easy" to parent. Neurodiverse children often notice very early when they seem harder to manage than a sibling, slower to finish work, more frequently corrected, or less naturally suited to school expectations.

A family strength narrative helps protect identity by making the family story broader, kinder, and more accurate. It does not pretend challenges are not real. It makes sure challenges are not the only thing being named.

Try this tonight

At dinner or bedtime, name one strength for each child that has nothing to do with grades, obedience, or speed. Keep it specific. Say, "You noticed your brother was upset and checked on him," or "You had a creative idea for solving that problem." This begins shifting the family spotlight from performance alone to the fuller child.

How to do it
  1. Notice the words your family uses most often. If one child is always "the organised one" and another is always "the difficult one," the story needs widening.
  2. Name strengths that are observable and specific — persistence, humour, curiosity, fairness, creativity, empathy, or courage.
  3. Acknowledge effort separately from outcome, especially when a child works hard for average results.
  4. Talk about different brains as needing different tools, not as better or worse versions of one another.
  5. Spend regular one-to-one time with each child so support for one child does not become invisibility for another.
What to say
Calm

"Different brains are good at different things and need different tools."

Warm

"You are not the hard child. You are a child having a hard time."

Firm

"In this family, we do not use one person's strength to shame another."

Why this works the science, briefly

Resilience research consistently highlights the importance of belonging, strengths recognition, supportive relationships, and identity development. For neurodiverse children, explicit strength-based language can counterbalance repeated correction and help build a more stable, accurate self-concept.

Skill Nine

Impulse Regulation &
The Pause

Parent Truth

It is very hard to stay regulated when a shoe has just flown past your head. Your regulation still comes first — not because you caused this, but because you are the one who can lead it out.

What this helps with

Impulse regulation is the ability to create space between a feeling and an action. For many neurodiverse children, that space is very small. The anger arrives and the hand moves. The thought appears and the words come out. The body runs before the thinking brain has properly arrived at the meeting.

The goal is not instant self-control. The goal is to help the child recognise earlier body signals, practise a predictable pause, and use a safer action before the impulse becomes behaviour.

Before you intervene

A calm adult is the first intervention. You cannot co-regulate a child in meltdown mode from inside your own meltdown.

When you feel your own reaction rising, before you say anything:

  1. Drop your shoulders. Lower your voice one level.
  2. Move slowly. A slow-moving adult reads as safe.
  3. Say less. One sentence. Same words, same tone, repeated once.

You do not need to be perfectly calm. You need to be calm enough. That is the whole job in that moment.

Try this tonight

Choose a calm moment and create a "pause plan" together. Ask, "What does your body feel like right before you explode?" Then choose one reset action your child can practise, such as squeezing a cushion, stepping away, drinking cold water, or asking for two minutes. Practise it when nobody is upset so the brain can find it later.

How to do it
Not this

Explaining consequences or reasoning through the behaviour while your child is in meltdown mode. The thinking brain has genuinely left the building. It will be back — wait for it before you have the conversation.

  1. Map the early body signs with your child — hot cheeks, tight fists, fast heartbeat, buzzing energy, or feeling trapped.
  2. Choose one pause signal. This can be a phrase such as "pause please," a hand signal, or a card kept somewhere visible.
  3. Choose one safe reset action. Keep it simple and physically available in the moment.
  4. Practise the sequence daily for one minute when calm: notice the signal, say the phrase, do the reset, return.
  5. After a real incident, review only when regulated. Ask, "Where could the pause have happened earlier?"
What to say
Calm

"Your body is giving warning signs. Let's catch them earlier."

Warm

"I know the feeling comes fast. We are practising the tiny space before action."

Firm

"Big feelings are allowed. Hands and words still need to be safe."

Why this works the science, briefly

Impulse regulation interventions commonly involve emotional literacy, early cue recognition, replacement behaviours, rehearsal during calm moments, reinforcement, and adult co-regulation. These strategies are consistent with behavioural parent training, CBT-informed regulation work, and social-emotional learning.

A note: Home strategies are helpful, but recurrent aggression, persistent self-harm statements, or unsafe behaviour should be discussed with a qualified psychologist, paediatrician, or relevant mental health professional.
